# California ML Feasibility for Predicting Unsafe Beach Bacteria and Closures

## Executive summary

A California-focused beach bacteria prediction system is technically feasible today, and the literature is mature enough to support a strong 48-hour hackathon prototype. The center of gravity of the field is still site-specific, interpretable tabular modeling rather than deep sequence modeling: tuned logistic regression, random forest, XGBoost/LightGBM-style boosting, and stacked ensembles dominate the best-documented FIB prediction studies, while California deployments have shown that daily nowcasts can materially improve sensitivity over the status quo of relying on previous-day or previous-week lab results. Rainfall, antecedent wetness, wave conditions, tidal phase, water temperature, salinity/turbidity proxies, and nearby streamflow/runoff are the most repeatedly useful predictors; for California specifically, the hardest but most novel edge case is the ŅłĆentityŅłé["place","Tijuana River","san diego county ca us"]Ņłü-influenced South Bay, where transboundary wastewater, storm pulses, and coastal transport interact. The strongest hackathon strategy is therefore a recall-optimized binary exceedance model for Enterococcus at California marine beaches, with E. coli treated as a secondary target for river mouths, lagoons, or creek-influenced sites where it is actually measured and operationally relevant. ## ML architecture recommendations

For a California hackathon build, the clearest evidence-backed baseline is a site-aware tabular classifier using boosted trees or a stacked ensemble, not an LSTM-first design. The strongest modern beach-water papers found in this review show LightGBM/XGBoost-class models and stacked ensembles outperforming or matching alternatives on precision/recall or annual accuracy, while the California deployment literature still uses tuned binary logistic regression and tuned regression not because they are glamorous, but because they are interpretable, robust under sparse labels, and easy to operationalize with managers. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34search0Ņłéturn34search4Ņłéturn34search1Ņłéturn34search5Ņłéturn33view1Ņłéturn35view1Ņłü

The features I would treat as mandatory are rainfall in multiple bins, not just raw daily totals; recent wave conditions; tide height and phase; annual seasonality; nearby streamflow or boundary flow; and one or more water-quality transport proxies such as salinity, turbidity, or conductivity. The California forecasting work explicitly engineered lags out to seven days and found value in meteorological, tide, wave, water-quality, streamflow, current, and date variables, while older Southern California work and broader coastal ML studies repeatedly identify rainfall, time since rainstorm, tide, wave height, wind, salinity, and outlet distance as strong signals. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view2Ņłéturn35view3Ņłéturn44search5Ņłéturn44search3Ņłéturn44search14Ņłü

For rainfall specifically, the literature supports retaining separate 0ŌĆō24 h, 24ŌĆō48 h, and 48ŌĆō72 h bins, plus longer cumulative totals for heavily urbanized systems. California practice still encodes this as the ŌĆ£72-hour rule,ŌĆØ but the mechanistic literature suggests the story is richer: runoff mobilizes fecal material from streets, drainage networks, outlets, creeks, lagoons, and river mouths; discharges can arrive at surf zones with delays; and local circulation can keep contamination elevated for three to five days or longer, depending on storm intensity and plume retention. In the reviewed California and related coastal sources, 72 hours is a sound operational feature window, while 3ŌĆō7 day cumulative rainfall and flow terms are often worth testing empirically. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn43search2Ņłéturn43search3Ņłéturn43search16Ņłéturn35view2Ņłéturn39search1Ņłü

I would therefore start with two modeling layers. The first should be a statewide pooled model with beach ID, latitude/longitude, beach class, and county as features, using XGBoost or LightGBM and time-blocked validation. The second should be a special South Bay / transboundary model for beaches near ŅłĆentityŅłé["city","Imperial Beach","california us"]Ņłü and ŅłĆentityŅłé["city","Coronado","california us"]Ņłü, where you add IBWC flow, tide, wave direction, and if possible nearshore current or plume proxies, because that region behaves more like a coastal transport problem than a simple rainfall/runoff problem. This is an inference from the California nowcast papers, the San Diego storm-pathogen paper, and the San DiegoŌĆōTijuana wastewater-transport work. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view1Ņłéturn35view2Ņłéturn39search1Ņłéturn37search3Ņłü

On deep learning, the reviewed beach/FIB literature is still thin. I did not find a California or West Coast beach FIB paper where LSTM or TFT had clearly displaced tree ensembles in operational relevance; the sequence-heavy frontier appears to be broader environmental forecasting rather than beach FIB specifically. That means an LSTM or TFT is best treated as an optional second-stage experiment if you can resample all predictors to a consistent hourly grid and create windows of one to seven days, but it should not be your first model in a 48-hour build. The current published beach benchmark frontier is moving toward more structured spatiotemporal models, including graph-based approaches, but those are not yet the California operational norm. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view2Ņłéturn36search19Ņłéturn46search12Ņłü

For explainability, SHAP is the best-supported option in this domain. The strongest concrete examples I found are Li et al. for tree ensembles and Grb─Źi─ć et al. for coastal enterococci/E. coli forecasting, where SHAP was used to rank predictors and interpret directionality; for your project, I would mirror that with a global bar chart, a beeswarm plot, and dependence plots for rainfall lag, salinity, tide, wave height, and IBWC flow. Those plots are exactly what judges and domain mentors will need to see to trust that the model is learning transport physics rather than noise. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34search4Ņłéturn44search14Ņłü

For rare-event training, the best directly relevant evidence in this review favors threshold tuning and explicit imbalance handling over headline accuracy. Xu et al. used adaptive synthetic sampling in a beach-water-quality context, while Searcy and Boehm tuned model thresholds to maximize sensitivity while maintaining a minimum specificity target; that is much closer to California public-health practice than optimizing raw accuracy. In practical terms, use blocked time splits, compute lag features only from past information, apply any oversampling only inside the training fold, and tune the decision threshold for high recall subject to an acceptable false-positive burden. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34search3Ņłéturn34search7Ņłéturn34search10Ņłü

## California context

## Identified gaps

The biggest gap is that there still does not appear to be a widely documented, openly reproducible, statewide California model that fuses public bacteriology, county advisory history, rain, waves, tide, and transboundary flow into one benchmarkable pipeline. California has operational nowcast activity and a growing forecast literature, but the reviewed publications remain site-specific, and the code that is public is research-grade and limited rather than a mature statewide benchmark suite. That means a hackathon project that produces a reproducible California ETL-plus-model stack would be filling a genuine infrastructure gap, not merely re-implementing a solved problem. This is an inference from the reviewed California papers and public repositories. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view1Ņłéturn35view2Ņłéturn47view0Ņłéturn47view1Ņłü

A second gap is methodological. The California literature I found is still dominated by tuned regression, classification trees, random forests, boosting, and stacked ensembles; deep sequence models such as LSTM and especially TFT are much less represented in beach FIB work than in broader environmental forecasting. In practice, that means there is an opportunity to test whether a carefully regularized temporal model adds value once you have aligned multi-source hourly data, but it also means that a simple, well-validated boosting model is currently the more defensible state-of-the-art baseline. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view1Ņłéturn35view1Ņłéturn34search1Ņłéturn34search0Ņłü

A third gap is target design for CaliforniaŌĆÖs southern border beaches. Existing systems do not fully unify bacterial exceedance prediction with wastewater plume transport severity, border-gauge flow, and county closure logic.
